This Isn't a Linter

Most review tools pattern-match against known good practices.
/fp works bottom-up. It starts with
"we need a notification service"
and finds the real problem:
"users need to know when something requires their attention."

Then it builds toward what the solution should look like —
based on what it actually needs to do, not what's familiar.

The Five Phases

1

Decompose

Strip the problem to fundamental truths. What problem are we actually solving? What are the hard, irreducible constraints? What would the solution look like with zero legacy and zero bias?

2

Surface Assumptions

Find what's been taken for granted. "PostgreSQL because we always use PostgreSQL." "REST because REST." Every unjustified assumption is a risk nobody priced in.

3

Reason Upward

Given only the real problem and real constraints, build toward what the solution should look like. Compare that against what was actually proposed or built.

4

Anchor Against Principles

Cross-check against 15 engineering principles — not as a checklist, but selectively based on what the analysis revealed. Separation of Concerns. Reversibility. Fail-Fast.

5

Produce Assessment

Direct findings with severity. Socratic questions for deeper exploration. Concrete recommendations. Plus what's working well — this isn't just a criticism tool.

What You Get

A real architecture review of an over-engineered e-commerce platform.

Critical — Cargo-Culted Architecture
You have 12 microservices, 4 developers, and hundreds of orders per day. The microservices architecture was adopted because "modern systems use microservices," but your actual constraints — modest scale, small team, rapid iteration — point to a well-structured monolith with clear module boundaries.
Principle: Simplicity · Reversibility
Moderate — Accidental Complexity
The distributed transactions you're fighting are accidental complexity. Eight of twelve services share a single database — you have the coordination overhead of microservices without the independence benefits. The coupling is hidden behind HTTP calls instead of function calls, making it slower and harder to debug.
Principle: Coupling & Cohesion
Working Well
The payment service is correctly isolated with its own data store and clear API contract. This is the one service that has genuinely different scaling, security, and compliance characteristics. Keep this boundary.
Explore Further
"If you could start over with what you know now, would you draw the same service boundaries? What would change if you organized around business domains instead of technical layers?"

15 Principles

Not a checklist. A reference. The skill draws from these selectively based on what the analysis reveals.

Foundational — Always Relevant

Separation of Concerns

"If requirement X changes, how many places break?"

Information Hiding

"Can I change internals without breaking consumers?"

Coupling & Cohesion

"Do things that change together live together?"

Simplicity

"What happens if I delete this?"

Single Source of Truth

"If this fact changes, how many places need updating?"

Structural — Architecture & Design

Least Knowledge

"Does this component know things it doesn't need?"

Reversibility

"What's the cost of changing this decision in 6 months?"

Dependency Direction

"Does business logic depend on infrastructure?"

Fail-Fast

"If this input is invalid, when does the system find out?"

Composition over Inheritance

"Am I inheriting behavior I don't need?"

Operational — Scale & Production

Observability

"If this breaks at 3am, can on-call understand it?"

Idempotency

"What happens if this runs twice?"

Graceful Degradation

"If dependency X dies, does everything stop?"

Backpressure

"What happens when load exceeds capacity?"

Least Astonishment

"Would a new team member be surprised by this?"

Philosophy

Reasoning > Checklists

A checklist catches known patterns. First-principles reasoning catches novel problems — the ones nobody thought to check for.

Assumptions Are Risk

The most dangerous technical decisions are the ones nobody realized they were making. Surface them before they surface themselves.

Assessment, Not Judgment

The goal is clarity about trade-offs, not a score. "Microservices" isn't wrong. "Microservices because microservices" is.
